## What Makes Cloud Hosting in Hong Kong Different

Hong Kong occupies a genuinely weird position in the global internet map. It's one of the best-connected cities in Asia — with HKIX, one of the largest internet exchange points in the region, sitting right there. Latency to Tokyo, Singapore, and Sydney is typically under 50ms. For regional Asia-Pacific businesses, it's hard to beat as a hosting location.

But here's where it gets complicated: a huge portion of Hong Kong's hosting demand comes from people who need reliable access *into mainland China*. And mainland China's internet infrastructure is... its own thing.

Standard international routing into China often means congestion, packet loss during peak hours, and latency spikes that make applications feel broken. The difference between a server running on generic transit and one running on a properly optimized China-facing route can be the difference between a 50ms response and a 400ms response at 9 PM on a Tuesday.

That's why cloud hosting HK isn't just a commodity purchase. Routing architecture matters enormously — and it's the first question worth asking any provider.

## The Three Connectivity Tiers You'll Actually Encounter

When you're evaluating cloud hosting HK providers, you'll eventually run into a few network terms. Here's what they actually mean in plain language:

**Standard International Transit (RETN, NTT, etc.)**
This is the default for most budget cloud hosting. Your traffic takes whatever path the internet decides. Fast enough for most international use cases; often sluggish or unstable for mainland China connections.

**CMI (China Mobile International)**
China Mobile's international network. Better than generic transit for reaching Chinese users, especially mobile. Decent latency, reasonable stability. Good middle-ground option.

**CN2 GIA (China Telecom Global Internet Access)**
This is the premium option. CN2 GIA is China Telecom's dedicated high-quality route — specifically engineered to avoid the congestion points that bog down standard internet paths. Low latency, high stability, and the closest thing to a predictable experience for China-bound traffic. It costs more, but if your users are in China, the performance difference is usually worth it.

Understanding these tiers makes it a lot easier to shop intelligently. You're not just buying vCPUs and RAM — you're buying a network path.
